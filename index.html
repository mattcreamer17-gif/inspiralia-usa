The US practice is pivoting into OTAs and Department of War capture alongside your NSF, NIH, and DoD work. That is a lot of institutional memory to keep aligned across nine offices.

The 60/30/10 frame

Most of what slows down a proposal cycle is not AI work. Roughly 60% of the throughput problem is file structure, version control, and retrieval: knowing which prior proposal had the best reviewer response to a novelty objection, which state-of-the-art paragraph is current, which technical framing won at NSF but failed at NIH. That is database and context work, not LLM work.

Another 30% is rule-based: deadline calendars, compliance checklists, audit triggers for the PMO arm. The last 10% is where language models actually belong. Adapting a prior technical narrative to a new client's specifics. Pattern-matching reviewer comments across a decade of submissions. Writing first drafts of recurring sections that a senior consultant then edits rather than writes.

The reason most consultancies waste money on AI is that they try to put LLMs where a filesystem and a rule engine would do the job faster, cheaper, and with auditable trails. The Inspiralia stack is a natural fit for this triage because your work is already structured around templates, agency-specific checklists, and recurring proposal patterns.

One relevant case study

VigilOre: 160+ hours of compliance review, now under 5 minutes

VigilOre is a compliance platform we built with a mining client in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Their senior compliance specialists were spending 160+ hours per regulatory cycle doing document review and response pattern-matching. We redesigned the knowledge layer as an Interpretable Context Methodology filesystem and built a multi-agent layer on top.

That 160-hour loop now runs in under 5 minutes, with full audit trails. The structural shape is the same as what happens inside Inspiralia every time a senior consultant starts a new proposal against an agency they have written 40 proposals for.
